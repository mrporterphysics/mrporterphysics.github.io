# Correlation vs. Causation

<div class="vocab">

**Correlation** — Two things change together, but we haven't shown one causes the other.

**Causation** — We have evidence that one thing causes the other through a physical mechanism.

</div>

<div class="key-idea">

**CCC2:** *Empirical evidence is required to differentiate between cause and correlation and to make claims about specific causes and effects.*

</div>

Right now we have **correlation**:
- The climate rapidly cooled ~13,000 years ago
- The Clovis culture disappeared around the same time

We need **evidence of a mechanism** before we can claim causation.

# The investigation, simply

We're going to model the **Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)** in a tank.

- **Control tank** — cold, very salty water at one end; warm water at the other
- **"After glacial melt" tank** — same setup, but the cold water is *diluted* with fresh water

**We watch what happens to the circulation.**

<div class="warning">

**Our prediction needs to come from density reasoning:** How does adding fresh water change the salinity — and therefore the density — of the cold water that's supposed to sink?

</div>

<!--
TEACHER MOVE: Have students plan the setup BEFORE building. Leverage middle-school knowledge of ocean circulation — cold sinks, warm rises, salty is denser. If it doesn't surface, show the AMOC Model video.
CONFERRING QUESTIONS (during planning): "How will an influx of freshwater affect the salinity of the ocean at the north pole?" "How would you model that change?" "How would a change in salinity affect the current compared to the control?"
EXPECTED STUDENT IDEAS: "Glacier water is fresh, ocean water is salty." "Lower salinity means lower density." "If the water doesn't sink as fast, the current slows."
-->

---

# Key vocabulary for Explore 1

<div class="vocab">

**Salinity** — The concentration of dissolved salts in water (measured in parts per thousand, ppt). Seawater averages ~35 ppt; glacial meltwater is near 0 ppt.

**Density** — Mass per unit volume. In ocean water, density increases with colder temperature AND with higher salinity. Both matter.

**Thermohaline circulation** — Ocean circulation driven by differences in *thermal* (temperature) and *haline* (salinity) conditions.

**AMOC** — Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation. The part of the global conveyor where warm surface water moves north, cools, becomes saltier, sinks near Greenland, and returns south along the ocean floor.

</div>

<!--
TEACHER MOVE: Introduce these terms as students encounter them in the investigation, not as a front-loaded vocabulary list. "Salinity" especially — students will use "salt concentration" and "salinity" interchangeably and that's fine.
DIFFERENTIATION: For multilingual learners, pair the term with both the scientific definition and a concrete example from the tank model.
-->

---

# What the data shows

<div class="columns">
<div class="col">

**Control ("before" melt)**

- Cold, very salty water sinks quickly
- Clear convection cell forms
- Current is steady and fast
- Represents pre-industrial AMOC

</div>
<div class="col">

**Freshwater added ("after" melt)**

- Cold water sinks much more slowly
- Convection cell forms sluggishly
- Current is weaker
- At high dilution, may not form at all

</div>
</div>

<div class="key-idea">

**The mechanism, empirically confirmed:** Reducing salinity reduces density. Less-dense water can't sink as easily. Without sinking, the current slows or stops.

</div>

<!--

CONFERRING QUESTIONS: "What do you notice about the current in your control?" "How does it change when you decrease salt concentration?" "What would happen if we kept decreasing?"
LOOK & LISTEN FOR: "When we decreased the amount of salt at the poles, the current formed but it took longer to form and moved more slowly." "When we increased the salt, the current formed more quickly." "This could have happened in the past when ice sheets were retreating and expanding." "Currents would keep slowing — or stop — if glacial melt continued."
-->

# Part 1 — The mechanism, in a model

**A strong student model links these components:**

1. Warming climate → ice sheets melt
2. Meltwater flows into the North Atlantic
3. Salinity drops at the AMOC sink zone
4. Lower salinity → lower density (even when cold)
5. Water can't sink → downwelling slows
6. AMOC conveyor slows
7. Less warm water pulled north
8. Less heat delivered to high latitudes
9. Northern hemisphere cools

<div class="warning">

**Timeframe check:** Decades to a few centuries — *much faster* than orbital forcing.

</div>

<!--
TEACHER MOVE: Confer with groups as they develop models. Look for arrows and labels linking density → sinking → current speed → heat transport. Push back on models that only describe "current slows" without the density mechanism.
CONFERRING QUESTIONS: "What happened to the salt concentration? What caused that?" "How does your model show that?" "How does that affect the current? What evidence do you have?"
COMMON MISCONCEPTION: Students may say "freshwater floats because it's less dense" but miss that the IMPORTANT density is of the COLD water at the sink zone. Make sure they're reasoning about the specific water parcel that's supposed to sink.
-->

# Part 2 — How currents carry energy

<div class="columns">
<div class="col">

**Why water matters so much:**

- Very high **heat capacity** — stores huge amounts of thermal energy
- High **density** — large mass per volume
- Together: the ocean is a massive thermal reservoir

</div>
<div class="col">

**How heat moves:**

- **Conduction** — direct contact
- **Convection** — bulk fluid motion (the dominant mode)
- **Radiation** — electromagnetic waves

</div>
</div>

<div class="key-idea">

Ocean currents transport energy from the equator to high latitudes. The Gulf Stream is the reason **Western Europe is milder than its latitude would predict.**

</div>

<!--
TEACHER MOVE: Read the "Ocean Heat Storage and Transfer" text together. Use the annotation strategy (✓ connects to my ideas; ○ answers my question; ? unclear). Have partners share annotations, then discuss as a class.
KEY POINTS: (1) Water's high heat capacity = it's a thermal buffer. (2) High density = small volume holds lots of energy. (3) Heat transfer happens from hot to cold, and stops when temperatures equalize. (4) The dominant heat-transfer mode between water and air is convection (including evaporation/condensation latent heat).
LOOK & LISTEN FOR: "AMOC transports water from near the equator toward the Arctic." "Water has high heat capacity, so it stores a lot of energy." "Heat transfers from hot to cold." "Evaporation carries kinetic energy with it."
-->

# Global ocean current pattern

<div class="columns">
<div class="col">

**Warm currents**
- Move from equator toward poles
- Flow along eastern sides of ocean basins
- Warm the air above → warm coastal climates
- Example: Gulf Stream warming Western Europe

</div>
<div class="col">

**Cold currents**
- Move from poles toward equator
- Flow along western sides of ocean basins
- Cool the air above → cool coastal climates
- Example: California Current keeping SF cool in summer

</div>
</div>

# Part 3 — The Clovis collapse, explained

1. Earth warms; ice sheets melt rapidly (~15,000 yr ago)
2. Meltwater pulses — including glacial Lake Agassiz — flood the North Atlantic
3. Salinity drops at the AMOC sink zone
4. AMOC slows (maybe shuts down) — the **Younger Dryas** begins
5. Less heat reaches North America
6. Temperatures plummet within decades (~12,900 yr ago)
7. Megafauna and plant communities disrupted
8. **Clovis culture disappears from the fossil record (~12,750 yr ago)**
9. AMOC restarts (~11,500 yr ago); warmth returns quickly

<div class="key-idea">

From **correlation** to **causation** — each link has independent evidence (ice cores, meltwater geology, physical tank model, archaeological record).

</div>

<!--
TEACHER MOVE: Have students use The End of the Clovis Population Cards to build this sequence in groups. Then ask them to write the causal explanation in their handouts.
3D INTEGRATION: This is the capstone where SEP2 (modeling), DCI ESS2.D (weather & climate), and CCC2 (cause & effect with empirical evidence) converge. Elevate that this is a causal story with independent evidence at every link — not just correlation.
DIFFERENTIATION: Struggling students can physically arrange the cards in sequence before writing. Advanced students can be asked what additional evidence would further strengthen the causal argument.
-->

# Class Consensus — key points to surface

- Ocean currents **transport solar energy** absorbed in the ocean around the globe
- AMOC carries warm water north, where it cools and becomes saltier
- **Colder and saltier = denser**
- Melting glaciers released fresh water where AMOC sinks
- Salinity controls density and sinking → AMOC would have slowed
- Less heat reaching northern latitudes → temperatures dropped
- As warm air built up in the Southern Hemisphere and moved north, temperatures rebounded
- The warm-cold-warm whiplash created floods and disrupted wildlife → Clovis loss

# The sea level record — 32 years of data

<div class="col">

**Overall trend**

- Clear, consistent rise
- Total: ~96 mm over 32 years

**Seasonal variation**

</div>
<div class="col">

**Rates (calculated):**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Seasonal monthly change | ~2 mm/month |
| Annual rate (32-yr avg) | **~3.0 mm/yr** |
| Linear 50-year projection | ~150 mm (~6 in) |
| Linear 100-year projection | ~300 mm (~12 in) |

</div>
</div>

# But the rate is accelerating

<div class="warning">

**The linear extrapolation is a floor, not a ceiling.**

- The 32-year average rate (~3 mm/yr) undershoots recent years
- Ice sheet feedbacks (especially in Antarctica and Greenland) are adding non-linearity
- IPCC projections for 2100 range from ~30 cm (low emissions) to **more than 1 m** (high emissions)

</div>

> **Key question for Part 2:** How do different future emissions scenarios change the numbers?

<!--

-->

# IPCC warming scenarios — what the "SSPs" mean

<div class="vocab">

**SSP** = Shared Socioeconomic Pathway — a scenario for future emissions based on assumptions about economic growth, technology, and climate policy.

|----------|-------------------------|---------|
| **SSP1-2.6** | +1.8 °C | Aggressive mitigation, low emissions |
| **SSP2-4.5** | +2.7 °C | Middle of the road |
| **SSP3-7.0** | +3.6 °C | Regional rivalry, high emissions |
| **SSP5-8.5** | +4.4 °C | Fossil-fueled development, very high emissions |

# Reading the data — what to look for

| Variable | Under SSP1-2.6 | Under SSP5-8.5 |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| Sea level rise (global avg) | ↑ modest (~30 cm) | ↑ large (>1 m) |
| Temperature | ↑ +1.8 °C | ↑ +4.4 °C |
| Precipitation | regional shifts | more extremes |
| Sea ice | ↓ substantial | ↓ dramatic |

<div class="warning">

**Rates scale faster than magnitudes.** Moving from SSP1-2.6 to SSP5-8.5 roughly doubles the temperature rise — but MORE than doubles the sea level rise and the rate of change, because of ice-sheet feedbacks.

# Glacial Lakes — the GLOF mechanism

<div class="vocab">

**GLOF** (Glacial Lake Outburst Flood) — when a natural rock dam holding back glacial meltwater suddenly fails, sending a catastrophic wall of water downstream.

</div>

**How it works:**

1. Glacier melts; meltwater pools behind a rock-and-moraine dam
2. Water level rises year by year
3. Earthquake, erosion, or overtopping breaches the dam
4. Valley below is devastated in minutes to hours

<div class="warning">

**15 million people worldwide** are vulnerable to GLOFs. The hazard is growing as glaciers retreat faster.

</div>

# The Poles — feedback loops and collapse

# 📚 Vocabulary reference (1 of 2)

<div class="vocab">

**AMOC** — Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation; the North Atlantic portion of the global ocean conveyor belt.

**Albedo** — The fraction of solar radiation reflected by a surface. Ice has high albedo; open water has low albedo.

**Causation** — A relationship where one thing actually produces another, via a physical mechanism. Requires empirical evidence.

**Correlation** — A statistical relationship between two variables; does not by itself imply causation.

**Density** — Mass per unit volume; in seawater, increases with cold temperature AND high salinity.

**Feedback (positive)** — A response that amplifies the original change (e.g., ice-albedo feedback).

**Feedback (negative)** — A response that dampens the original change (e.g., increased evaporation cooling a surface).

</div>

---

# 📚 Vocabulary reference (2 of 2)

<div class="vocab">

**GLOF** — Glacial Lake Outburst Flood; catastrophic failure of a natural meltwater dam.

**Heat capacity** — Amount of energy required to raise a unit mass by one degree. Water's is very high.

**Meltwater pulse** — A rapid release of glacial meltwater into the ocean.

**Salinity** — Concentration of dissolved salts in water, measured in ppt (parts per thousand).

**SSP (Shared Socioeconomic Pathway)** — An IPCC scenario for future emissions based on socioeconomic assumptions.

**Thermohaline circulation** — Ocean circulation driven by both temperature (thermo-) and salinity (haline) differences.

**Younger Dryas** — Abrupt cooling event ~12,900–11,700 years ago, linked to AMOC slowdown from meltwater input.

</div>
