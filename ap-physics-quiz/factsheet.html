1. Kinematics

1.1 Kinematic Definitions

Displacement (Δx or Δy): Indicates how far an object ends up from its initial position, regardless of its total distance traveled.
Average velocity (v̄): Displacement divided by the time interval over which that displacement occurred.
Instantaneous velocity (v): How fast an object is moving at a specific moment in time.
Average Speed: Total distance divided by time duration.

1.2 Position-time graphs

Reading position-time graphs:
  • Vertical axis shows distance from detector
  • Slope indicates speed (steepness = how fast)
  • For curved graphs, use tangent line slope for instantaneous speed
Direction from slope:
  • Front slash (/) slope → moving away from detector
  • Back slash (\) slope → moving toward detector
Acceleration from shape:
  • Concave up (U shape) → positive acceleration
  • Concave down (∩ shape) → negative acceleration

1.3 Velocity-time graphs

Reading velocity-time graphs:
  • Vertical axis shows speed
  • Above horizontal axis → moving away from detector
  • Below horizontal axis → moving toward detector
  • Area under curve = displacement
  • Slope = acceleration
Note: On velocity-time graphs, you cannot determine the object's location.

1.4 Acceleration

Acceleration: How much an object's speed changes in one second.
  • Speeding up → acceleration in direction of motion
  • Slowing down → acceleration opposite direction of motion
  • Free fall acceleration: g ≈ 10 m/s² (near Earth's surface)
  • Units: m/s² or m/s/s

1.5 Special equations for displacement

Constant speed: Δx = vt
From rest or to rest: x = ½at² or x = v₀t + ½at²
The constant speed equation includes horizontal components of projectile motion.

1.6 Algebraic kinematics

Problem-solving steps:
  1. Define positive direction (e.g., "away from detector")
  2. Specify motion portion being analyzed
  3. Choose appropriate kinematic equation
  4. Substitute known values
  5. Solve algebraically
Key kinematic equations:
• v = v₀ + at
• x = v₀t + ½at²
• v² = v₀² + 2ax
• x = ½(v₀ + v)t

1.7 Projectile motion

Key principles:
  • Horizontal acceleration = 0 (no air resistance)
  • Vertical acceleration = g ≈ 10 m/s²
  • Horizontal and vertical motions are independent
  • Time of flight determined by vertical motion only
Velocities add using Pythagorean theorem: v = √(vₓ² + vᵧ²)

1.8 Rotational kinematics

Angular displacement (θ): Angle through which an object rotates (radians)
Angular velocity (ω): Rate of angular displacement (rad/s)
Angular acceleration (α): Rate of change of angular velocity (rad/s²)
Linear-angular relationships: v = rω, a = rα

2. Dynamics

2.1 Definition of Equilibrium

An object is in equilibrium if it is either:
  • At rest, or
  • Moving in a straight line at constant speed
Equilibrium means the net force on the object is zero (ΣF = 0).

2.2 Newton's Second Law

F = ma
  • Net force is in the direction of acceleration
  • F and a are vectors pointing in the same direction
  • Force causes acceleration, not velocity

2.3 Solving problems with forces

Problem-solving strategy:
  1. Draw free-body diagram
  2. Choose coordinate system
  3. Apply Newton's second law in each direction
  4. Solve the resulting equations

2.4 Mass and Weight

Mass (m): Intrinsic property, measured in kg
Weight (W): Force due to gravity, measured in N
W = mg

2.5 Normal force

Normal force (N): Contact force perpendicular to surface
Normal force does NOT always equal weight. It equals the component of all forces perpendicular to the surface.

2.6 Adding perpendicular forces

Pythagorean theorem: F = √(Fₓ² + Fᵧ²)
Angle: tan θ = Fᵧ/Fₓ

2.8 Free-body diagrams

A free-body diagram includes:
  • Each arrow begins on the object
  • Each arrow points in direction force acts
  • Only forces acting ON the object
  • No motion arrows or acceleration arrows

2.9 Inclined planes

Weight parallel to incline: mg sin θ
Weight perpendicular to incline: mg cos θ
Use coordinate system aligned with the incline for easier calculations.

2.10 Friction

Friction force: f = μN
μₛ: Coefficient of static friction (higher)
μₖ: Coefficient of kinetic friction (lower)
Friction opposes motion and acts parallel to the surface.

2.11 Newton's Third Law

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
Important:
  • Action-reaction pairs act on DIFFERENT objects
  • Forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction
  • They occur simultaneously

3. Circular motion

3.1 Circular motion basics

An object moving in a circle at constant speed still has acceleration because its velocity direction constantly changes.

3.2 Centripetal acceleration

aᶜ = v²/r
Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of the circle.

3.3 Centripetal force

Fᶜ = maᶜ = mv²/r
Centripetal force is not a new type of force - it's the net force toward the center provided by tension, gravity, friction, etc.
