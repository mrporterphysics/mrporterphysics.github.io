Editor's Letter

There is a rule nature refuses to break. Push a spinning thing, pull its mass inward, stretch its arms into the cold — and the universe will keep its books balanced. Angular momentum in equals angular momentum out. No exceptions, no bargaining, no clever loopholes.

This issue is about that rule. You will meet it in an ice rink and again in a dying star. You will learn to write it down in one line, use it in one equation, and recognize the four moments when it rules the room. It is, quite possibly, the most elegant thing you will learn all year.

— N. Porter, Editor

03 — The LawConservation
§ 01 · First Principle

When no external torque acts on a system, its total angular momentum stays constant.

Notice the loophole: no external torque. Internal forces can rearrange mass all they like. They can pull, push, squeeze, and fling. As long as nothing outside the system twists it, the bookkeeping stands.

05 — The SkaterCase Study
Fig. 05 — Mass withdrawn to the axis. Spin multiplies accordingly.
Case Study · 05

The skater who cheats nothing.

Arms out, a skater rotates slowly. Arms in, she becomes a blur. The audience calls it grace. Physics calls it bookkeeping.

By drawing her mass toward the axis, she cuts her moment of inertia to perhaps a third of what it was. The ice exerts almost no torque on her. Therefore L = Iω must stay put — so ω must grow by the same factor. Her spin triples because it has nowhere else to go.

08 — Worked ProblemPractice
Free Response · Exemplar

A student on a stool.

A student sits on a frictionless rotating stool holding two 2-kg dumbbells at arm's length, 0.80 m from the rotation axis. The stool-student-dumbbell system has a base inertia of 3.0 kg·m² and rotates at 1.2 rad/s. The student pulls the dumbbells inward to 0.20 m from the axis. Find the new angular speed.

Target: L conserved · no external torque
Initial Inertia
I₁ = 3.0 + 2(2)(0.8)² = 5.56
Final Inertia
I₂ = 3.0 + 2(2)(0.2)² = 3.16
Conservation
I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂
Answer
ω₂ ≈ 2.11 rad/s

09 — PitfallsDon't Do This

Four mistakes
the exam
loves.Memorize at your peril

i.
Confusing energy with momentum.

Angular momentum is conserved in the skater. Rotational kinetic energy is not — it actually increases, because the skater does work pulling her arms in. Two different quantities. Two different rules.

ii.
Forgetting to check for external torque.

Conservation only applies when net external torque is zero. Friction, gravity on an off-axis point, a hand on the wheel — any of them breaks the law. Always justify that τ_ext = 0 before invoking I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂.

iii.
Using linear intuition on rotating mass.

Doubling the distance doesn't double the inertia — it quadruples it. The r² is not decoration. Where mass sits matters far more than how much of it there is.

iv.
Treating ω as a scalar on a whim.

Angular momentum is a vector. In collisions and mergers on the AP exam, direction matters: two disks spinning the opposite way can cancel each other out. Pick a positive sense before you start.
