02 — OpeningThe Balance Issue

“Every object has a quiet point where all of its mass pretends to live.”

Editor's Letter

There is an address where your body's mass, for all of Newton's purposes, is filed. It is not your belt buckle, though it's nearby. It moves when you raise your arms. It leaves your body entirely when you arch backwards to clear a high-jump bar. Find it, and you can treat yourself — or a car, or a galaxy — as a single particle.

The quantity is called the center of mass. It is the weighted average of where all the mass of a system sits, and it is the object that Newton's second law was secretly written about all along. F_net = ma is a promise about this one point. Everything else is noise around it.

This issue teaches you to compute it, to trust it, and to use it to answer problems that look impossible from any other angle.

— N. Porter, Editor

§ 01 · First Principle

A weighted average of position. Nothing more.

Every particle contributes to the total in proportion to its own mass. A heavy particle pulls the answer hard; a light one barely whispers. Add up the pulls, divide by the total mass, and you have the address of the whole system.

04 — Why It MattersThe Shortcut

One point, one law.

Feature · 04
Fext = Macm

The promiseNo matter how complicated the internal motion — limbs flailing, parts colliding, pieces rotating — the center of mass moves as if the whole mass were a single particle there.

Internal forcesThey never appear in this equation. Pushes and pulls between the parts of a system cancel in pairs. The only thing that can accelerate the center of mass is a force from outside.

The consequenceIf no outside force acts, the center of mass cruises at constant velocity forever. A closed system can rearrange itself in every way — and its center of mass never wavers.

Page FourNewton, quietly
Case Study · 05

A person walks across a boat.

A person stands at one end of a stationary boat floating on still water. There is no friction between the boat and the water. The person walks to the other end. Where does the boat end up?

No outside horizontal force acts on the person-boat system. That means the center of mass cannot move. If the person shifts right, the boat must shift left by whatever amount keeps the weighted average of position fixed. The heavier the boat, the less it shifts; the heavier the person, the more.

For a 60-kg person on a 120-kg, 4.0-m boat, the boat slides backwards by exactly 4⁄3 m while the person walks forward relative to the water. Not a trick — an accounting identity.

06 — Three Wild PlacesField Guide
Field Guide · The CM Shows Up
Hero Case · 01
i.

The high jumper whose center of mass never clears the bar.

A Fosbury Flop athlete arches backwards over the bar. As she does, her limbs and torso form a shape whose center of mass sits below her body. The bar passes through that empty region. Her body goes over — her center of mass passes under. The energy required is a fraction of what a jump with an upright body would cost, and it is one of the most beautiful applications of a weighted average in all of sport.

ii.
Shape · 02
The donut.

A ring or a boomerang has its center of mass in its hollow center — a point where no part of the object exists. Newton doesn't mind. The weighted average is where it is whether or not mass sits there to occupy it.

iii.
Cosmos · 03
The binary star.

Two stars do not orbit each other. They both orbit their common center of mass — a point suspended between them, leaning toward the heavier one. If the masses are equal, the point is exactly halfway. If one star is a hundred times heavier, the point sits just outside its own surface.

07 — By The NumbersEarth & Moon
Feature Stat · The Earth-Moon Barycenter
4,671km
What

The distance from the center of the Earth to the center of mass of the Earth-Moon system — the point about which they both orbit.

Where

Inside the Earth. The planet's radius is 6,371 km, so the barycenter sits about 1,700 km beneath the surface on the side facing the Moon at any given moment.

Why

It's the weighted average of two masses, one roughly 81 times larger than the other. The heavier body pulls the point close to itself — but not quite to its own center.

08 — Worked ProblemPractice
Free Response · Exemplar

Three masses, one line.

Three small blocks sit on a frictionless rail along the x-axis. Block A (3.0 kg) is at x = 0 m. Block B (2.0 kg) is at x = 2.0 m. Block C (5.0 kg) is at x = 6.0 m. Find the x-coordinate of the system's center of mass.

Strategy: plug into x_cm = ∑mᵢxᵢ / M
Total Mass
M = 3 + 2 + 5 = 10 kg
Weighted Sum
∑mᵢxᵢ
= (3)(0) + (2)(2) + (5)(6)
= 0 + 4 + 30 = 34
Divide
x_cm = 34 / 10
Answer
3.4 m
Closer to C, as expected.

09 — PitfallsDon't Do This

Four mistakes
the exam
loves.Balance edition

i.
Averaging positions without the masses.

The center of mass is not the midpoint. It is a weighted average. Two masses at x = 0 and x = 10 don't have their CM at x = 5 unless the masses happen to be equal. Always multiply position by mass before adding.

ii.
Letting internal forces do work on the CM.

A person pushing off the inside wall of a canoe does not accelerate the canoe-person system. Internal forces can rearrange the pieces all they like — they cannot change the velocity of the center of mass. Only an external force can.

iii.
Assuming the CM lies inside the object.

For rings, horseshoes, boomerangs, and L-shaped brackets, the center of mass is in a location where no mass exists. That is perfectly legal. The formula doesn't require the answer to sit on top of matter.

iv.
Forgetting that x, y, and z are separate.

A 2D system has both an x_cm and a y_cm, each computed independently with its own weighted sum. Mixing coordinates — using a distance from the origin instead of a signed position — is a favorite trap on multi-choice items.

10 — End MatterBack Cover
A system's quiet point.

The center of mass is the one place where a complicated object agrees to behave simply. Find it, track it, and let Newton handle the rest. The three-mass problem you just solved is, at root, the same arithmetic that tells two stars where to orbit.
